General Tips [Go section by Section]

Finding Colleges

Learning about colleges

  • Since you can’t visit all your colleges, read about them in the Fiske guide [this is a mirror for a PDF, but you can also probably find it at the library]
  • If you are interested in getting statistics or filtering with more granularity, look at CollegeBoard Big Future or Naviance’s Advanced College Search
  • You can also try places like CollegeAI or Naviance’s SuperMatch -- I don’t really know what this is like
  • Most college websites are useless until you dig deep into the specific things that are going on
  • Researching college subreddits can be useful
  • Acceptance % is very misleading — the process isn’t random. Talk to people who got in/research what students at those colleges are like if you want to evaluate your chances.
    • Note colleges that you’re applying to that yield protect
    • Acceptance % (and GPA and avg. SAT etc) vary wildly with school, major, and application plan
  • Googling “[college] common data set” gives pdfs with lots of info about admissions processes (things like how much they value certain aspects of applications) 
  • If your school uses naviance or something like it, look at admissions history of past students
    • In naviance, you can use scattergrams or the acceptance history option
      • The data may be kind of skewed, though, as many schools have become more competitive with time & the data is self-reported
  • Generally Naviance has a surprising amount of info
    • Use college compare, look at individual colleges, 

Comparing Financial Aid

  • Tuition reciprocity means you may not pay out of state tuition for some colleges. Here’s Ohio’s website on it.
  • Look at how much aid school would give you if, for example, you got something like National Merit Scholarship Finalist
  • Research college-specific scholarships and plan deadlines for those

Picking colleges

  • Work doesn’t increase linearly with # colleges because you can reuse information
  • In my mind there are three sizes that you should strive to pick from…
    • [This is a very broad generalization. Take it with a big grain.

Few colleges

Medium

Lots

3-4

  • You probably know the schools, so # supplementals isn’t a factor

5-10

  • # supplementals may be a factor
  • Can start early because it’s less stressful

10+

  • Don’t really consider # supplementals as heavily as you can probably reuse things & you’ve already committed to a lot of work
  • You need to start very early

Essays

  • Just get them done early
    • Generally speaking, the more time you have to revisit and re-edit them, the better
  • Common app essay prompts do not change, so don’t wait for them
  • Make a list of things that you want to show about yourself in your application
    • Make sure every single application you submit shows most if not all the things
    • Essays are a great way to add things
    • Have a “major theme” and a “minor theme” for each application. One way to think about it (not the only way) is: major theme characterizes you professionally/academically, minor theme shows why you’re different (personally) from other similar applicants.
      • Themes should be generally similar across colleges, but can differ based on supplemental prompts etc.
  • Copy, Paste, Edit
    • Start doing this early on
  • Have people read your essays and see what they say the main takeaway is
    • This is often different from what you intended
  • Think about how unique your essay is: of the other applicants to this school, how many could/would write this?
  • Make a Google Drive folder

Letters of Recommendation

  • Pick recommenders based on what unique (positive) perspectives they can give about you
    • Start building relationships with teachers, sooner rather than later
  • Give recommenders some info
    • List of colleges
    • The things you do in/out of school (what they don’t know)
    • Some of your essays
  • Some colleges require specific people to be your recommenders
    • For example, 1 Science/Math and 1 Humanities teacher
  • Many colleges have the option for extra recommenders, so maybe find one
  • Make a table:

School Name

  • What they require
  • E.g., 1 humanities 1 stem

Rec 1 [* if required]

  • What they say about you

Rec 2 [* if required]

  • What they say about you

  • What they say about you

Submitting Applications

  • Most colleges have early deadlines for special programs (engineering, pre-med, etc) and scholarships
  • Make use of REA/ED IF you have a good chance of getting in (like would consider the school a match)
    • Saves application $, essay-writing time, increases chances of acceptance and decreases stress
    • Most of these allow exceptions for state schools, schools w/ limited programs/scholarships, and rolling admissions as well
    • Disregard if you don’t really love any on your list that have REA/ED
  • As you submit applications & get emailed the applicant portal, login & look around
    • You’d be surprised what you can miss

Financial Aid

  • Check if your schools are need-blind [use their website, not just Wikipedia]
    • It’s probably still a good idea to apply for aid
  • EFC = Expected Family Contribution = How much your family is supposed to contribute
  • You could use the FAFSA4caster to estimate the EFC since the FAFSA doesn’t open until October 1
  • Compile all your documents (tax and otherwise)
  • Do the FAFSA first (this is usually all you need for state schools)
    • Some schools have requirements for being first the first college listed (especially state schools)
    • You can only include 10 schools initially
      • You can add more by filing a correction & only changing the colleges
  • Next, do the CSS profile
    • This asks for a little bit more information
  • Next, do IDOC
    • You’ll need to log in with your CollegeBoard FinAid ID which you get from the CSS profile
    • All colleges that require IDOC require CSS profile (but not the other way around)
    • Just look at the list of required documents at the top
    • Be careful that you don’t miss any colleges requirement
      • Especially unique documents like the Business/Farm supplement
  • Finally, look through the individual financial aid requirements for your school
    • Some school have their own document submission portals like Penn’ PFAS
    • Some schools have their own Financial Aid application like Princeton’s PFAA

Scholarships

  • 🤷‍♂️
  • Start early
    • A lot are due in the fall and winter when you may be working on your main applications
  • Most have some sort of letter of recommendation requirement, but it’s usually only 1 letter
  • Many scholarships sell your data. Be careful.
  • Ideally, do them after your college essays so you have polished essays you can submit to scholarships
  • There are many scholarship aggregators like fastweb, scholarship universe (if a university you apply to offers it), BigFuture [add more]
    • You need to wade through a lot of bad ones to get the ones worth applying for
  • It’s usually not worth your time applying for scholarships you are not the target for
    • They’ll find someone who is
  • Be careful when searching; some have weird requirements. 

Interviews

  • Start interviewing at colleges during the summer
    • Many colleges want you to request an interview
  • Look for questions on the college’s website for alumni, like this
    • Similar to with essays, have an idea of what you want the interviewer’s main takeaways to be (qualities etc.)
    • FULLY write out answers to possible questions
  • Make it a conversation, not a presentation
  • Have questions for the very end

Things to do after you apply


Deciding on colleges

  • Financial aid packages often contain loans
  • Before you accept anything, double check your application to make sure there is nothing that may be incorrect that has not been reported to the college
    • College can rescind your offer, though they usually don’t
    • You don’t want to have it be an issue later since most schools ask you to send transcripts and scores later
